# Problem-Solution Fit

Problem-solution fit means you understand a real user problem well enough to propose a solution that feels relevant, useful, and worth trying.

## The framework

### 1. User

Who exactly is the user?

Be specific.

Bad:
- Everyone building startups

Better:
- First-time founders in their 20s who want practical startup guidance but lack mentors and networks

### 2. Problem

What painful problem does the user face?

A strong problem is:

- Frequent
- Frustrating
- Expensive in time, money, or missed progress
- Already visible in current behavior

### 3. Current alternatives

What does the user do today?

Examples:

- Googling random advice
- Watching generic YouTube content
- Asking friends
- Using scattered notes
- Doing nothing

If there are no alternatives, there may be no real urgency.

### 4. Proposed solution

What are you offering that is better?

Your solution should be:

- Clear
- Simple
- Relevant
- Faster or easier than current alternatives

### 5. Evidence

What proof do you have?

Useful signals:

- Interviews
- Signups
- Replies
- Repeat usage
- Referrals
- Willingness to pay

Hope is not evidence.

## Quick test

You may have problem-solution fit when:

- The user is clear
- The problem is real
- Current alternatives are weak
- Your offer is understandable
- People respond with genuine interest or action

## Common mistakes

- Defining the user too broadly
- Solving a mild problem
- Falling in love with the product before understanding the pain
- Confusing compliments with demand
- Using features to hide lack of clarity

## Core point

You do not get problem-solution fit by building more.

You get it by understanding better.
